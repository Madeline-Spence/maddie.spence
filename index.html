How I See Myself.
How Others See Me.
A reflective analysis of identity, reputation, and intentional professional growth. Understanding the relationship between how we see ourselves and how others perceive us is one of the most important foundations of strong personal branding. This section explores that relationship across three key dimensions — and what I am actively doing to grow within each one.
○
Identity vs. Reputation — Core Values & Character

My Identity (How I See Myself)

I see myself as energetic, outgoing, hardworking, and genuinely supportive of those around me. I lead with positivity and believe that strong team culture begins with each individual's attitude and commitment to showing up fully — not just in output, but in presence, care, and accountability.

My Reputation (How Others See Me)

Feedback from peers and colleagues consistently described me as super positive, easy to talk to, a great listener, and someone who immediately puts others at ease. I am seen as a reliable, encouraging presence — someone people genuinely want on their team. This confirms that my core values are translating into visible, consistent behavior.

Gap Insight

The gap here is not a conflict — it is that others recognize strengths in me that I sometimes minimize. I tend to be harder on myself than the evidence warrants. This demonstrates that self-perception can be a limiting factor even when external reputation is strong, and it underscores why treating feedback as data — rather than flattery — is a professional skill in itself.

Moving Forward

I am working to internalize the reputation others have built of me — to trust it, act from it, and let it inform how I step into leadership. If others already see these qualities clearly, my next step is to own them with intention rather than waiting for more permission to lead.

Deeper Reflection: What Identity vs. Reputation Taught Me

Identity is who we believe we are; reputation is the consistent impression we leave on others over time. When they align, it signals authenticity. When they diverge, it reveals a growth edge. In my case, the gap was not one of contradiction but of confidence: others see more in me than I sometimes allow myself to claim. That insight has shifted how I approach professional environments — I now lead from what I know others already see, rather than waiting until I feel fully ready.

Communication Style

My Identity (How I See Myself)

I see myself as a thoughtful communicator who genuinely listens before responding. At the same time, I tend to overthink my delivery and sometimes hold back — particularly in high-stakes conversations involving conflict or direct disagreement, where I worry about being misunderstood.

My Reputation (How Others See Me)

Others consistently describe me as confident, outgoing, warm, and easy to talk to. I am seen as someone who communicates clearly and makes people feel genuinely heard. My external presence reads as far more assured and direct than I often feel internally — a meaningful disconnect between internal experience and external impact.

Gap Insight

The confidence others perceive is real — it shows up in how I engage, listen, and respond — but I do not always feel it internally. This gap reveals that my hesitation lives primarily in my own thinking, not in my actual performance. This demonstrates that internal anxiety does not always predict external effectiveness, and that growth here is fundamentally about mindset, not missing skills.

Moving Forward

I want to be more direct, take more initiative in difficult conversations, and trust my instincts rather than second-guessing my words. My goal is for my internal sense of clarity and confidence to fully match the communication style others already experience — closing the gap from the inside out.

Growth Mindset

What Growth Mindset Means to Me Professionally

A growth mindset is the belief that abilities and intelligence can be developed through effort, strategy, and openness to feedback — as opposed to a fixed belief that talent is static. This semester, I moved from understanding that concept intellectually to living it in practice.

Growth mindset showed up most clearly in how I engaged with feedback about my professional persona. When peers and instructors reflected qualities back to me that I had not fully claimed — confidence, reliability, leadership presence — my first instinct was to minimize them. A growth mindset required me to receive that feedback as useful data and ask: what would change if I actually believed this? That question has driven real shifts in how I communicate, how I lead, and how I see my own trajectory.

I have also seen growth mindset at work in my professional experience. At V!ZI Lab, I stepped into a Creative Lead Strategist role without a blueprint — and learned by doing, asking questions, and staying curious when the work was uncertain. As a math tutor, I helped a student move from a D to a B by approaching each session not as a performance but as a problem to solve together. Both reflect the same principle: growth happens when you stay open, stay humble, and keep showing up.

Growth Over the Semester

This semester fundamentally deepened my understanding of how identity and reputation intersect — and why that matters professionally. I became more intentional about how I communicate, more aware of the gap between how I feel internally and how I actually show up for others, and more committed to leading with consistency rather than waiting for certainty.

The most meaningful shift was recognizing that confidence — for me — is not about feeling ready. It is about trusting the evidence: the feedback, the results, and the reputation I have already earned. That reframe has changed how I walk into rooms, how I contribute to teams, and how I think about my own professional future.

Moving Forward

Going forward, I aim to step more fully into leadership, communicate with greater directness, and continue building a professional presence that is as clear and grounded on the inside as it appears on the outside. I want my internal narrative to match my external reputation — not because I am performing a version of myself, but because I have done the real work of understanding who I am.

I will carry the insights from this semester into every professional environment I enter — in internships, in team settings, and in the business career I am actively building — with intention, authenticity, and a genuine commitment to continuous growth.
